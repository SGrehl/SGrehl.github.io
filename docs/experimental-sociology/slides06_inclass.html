Errors

Win-Stay-Lose-Shift

The Win-Stay, Lose-Shift (WSLS) strategy â€” represented by the bit string 11001 â€” begins with cooperation and then sticks with its current behavior if the outcome is favorable, and switches if it is not. Specifically, it continues cooperating if it receives either of the high-payoff outcomes: \(b\) (when the other cooperates while it defects) or \(b - c\) (mutual cooperation). If it receives one of the low-payoff outcomes â€” either \(0\) (mutual defection) or \(-c\) (it cooperates while the other defects) â€” it switches its action in the next round.

WSLS has a nice property in comparison to GT and TFT:

GT vs GT

  • When Grim Trigger (GT) plays against itself, a single mistake can lead to eternal defection:
    • Example:
      • Player A: \(C, C, D^*, C, D, D, D, \dots\)
      • Player B: \(C, C, C, D, D, D, D, \dots\)
    • After one accidental defection (\(D^*\)), both players switch to permanent defection, as GT never forgives once trust is broken.
    • The only way cooperation can resume is if both agents simultaneously make an error and accidentally cooperate.

TFT vs TFT

  • Tit-for-Tat (TFT) is slightly more forgiving, but still vulnerable to cycles of retaliation:
    • Example:
      • Player A: \(C, C, D^*, C, D, C, D, \dots\)
      • Player B: \(C, C, C, D, C, D, C, \dots\)
    • The mistake causes a chain reaction of alternating \(C\) and \(D\).
    • A second error at the right time can either restore mutual cooperation (\(C, C\)) or plunge the system into mutual defection (\(D, D\)).
    • In the latter case only simultaneous errors can save the cooperation.

WSLS vs WSLS

  • Win-Stay, Lose-Shift (WSLS) shines in noisy environments by actively correcting mistakes:
    • Example:
      • Player A: \(C, C, D^*, C, D, C, C, \dots\)
      • Player B: \(C, C, C, D, D, C, C, \dots\)
    • After a mistake, both players switch behavior based on the payoff they received.
    • This leads to a quick return to cooperation â€” typically within just a few rounds.
    • WSLS is thus robust to isolated errors and can maintain cooperation even when mistakes are frequent.
