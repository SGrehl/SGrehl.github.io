Basic Randomization Techniques

At its core, randomization is a tool for ensuring internal validity. It helps us isolate the causal effect of a treatment by addressing a key challenge in empirical research: unobserved heterogeneity.

In real-world data, individuals (or more general units) differ in countless observable and unobservable waysâ€”age, motivation, experience, attitudes, prior exposure, etc. If these characteristics are correlated with treatment assignment, any difference in outcomes could be due to selection bias, not the treatment itself.

Randomization solves this problem by:

  • Breaking the link between pre-existing characteristics and treatment assignment.

  • Ensuring that treatment and control groups are statistically equivalent in expectation.

  • Making it valid to attribute any post-treatment difference in outcomes to the treatment itself.

This allows researchers to infer causality, not just correlation.

The Core Principle

In all forms of randomization, the basic idea is the same:

Every unit has a known, non-zero probability of being assigned to any treatment condition.

In most experimental designs, this means that each unit has the same chance of being assigned to the treatment or control group. This uniform probability guarantees fairness and eliminates systematic bias in treatment assignment.

Importantly, this does not mean that the treatment effect will be perfectly measured in every sampleâ€”but it does mean that across many repetitions, the estimate will be unbiased and consistent.

Simple Randomization

One of the most straightforward randomization techniques is simple randomization. Here, each unit is assigned to a treatment or control condition completely independently of all others, typically with a 50/50 probability. This mirrors the process of flipping a fair coin for each participant.

The main advantages of this method are:

  • Itâ€™s conceptually simple.

  • Each assignment is independent.

  • There is no need to track previous assignments.

simple_ra(N=100) |> 
  table()

 0  1 
45 55

However, the simplicity comes at a cost: imbalance is likely. As seen in the output above, even with 100 participants, we might not get a perfectly balanced allocation. This imbalance reduces statistical power and can lead to less precise estimates, particularly in smaller samples.

Complete Randomization

To avoid the imbalance problem of simple randomization, we can use complete randomization. This method assigns units to conditions while enforcing equal or near-equal group sizes.

complete_ra(N=100) |> 
  table()

 0  1 
50 50

Complete randomization ensures that group sizes are as equal as mathematically possible. If N is not divisible by the number of conditions, the remaining units are distributed randomly across the groups.

For example, with three treatment arms:

complete_ra(N = 100, 
            conditions = c("control", "treatment 1", "treatment 2")
            ) |> 
  table()

    control treatment 1 treatment 2 
         33          33          34

As you can see, the 100 units are divided as evenly as possible across the three conditions (e.g., 33, 33, 34). The slight imbalance due to rounding is distributed randomly.

When feasible, prefer complete randomization over simple randomizationâ€”especially in small to medium-sized experimentsâ€”because better balance leads to better power and clearer interpretation.

Advanced Randomization Techniques

Block Randomization

Block randomization (also known as stratified randomization) is a powerful method to ensure balance across treatment and control groups along key covariatesâ€”such as gender, age, or region. It is particularly helpful in small to moderate sample sizes, where imbalance due to chance can significantly reduce statistical power.

Imagine you want to test a treatment in Germany, where 80% of people live in the west and only 20% in the east. Suppose regional background influences the outcome youâ€™re measuring (e.g., political preferences or economic outlook). If you assign treatment using complete randomization, the eastern subgroupâ€”being smallâ€”could easily end up unevenly distributed between treatment and control. This would introduce unnecessary variance into your estimates, making it harder to detect a real effect.

With block randomization, you can avoid this issue by dividing units into blocks (e.g., by region) and randomizing within each block. This preserves random assignment while ensuring group balance on important covariatesâ€”thus improving precision and power.

Letâ€™s see this in action:

df <- tibble(
    west       = rbinom(100, 1, 0.8),
    assignment = block_ra(west)
  )

with(df, table(assignment, west))
          west
assignment  0  1
         0 11 39
         1 10 40

As you can see, block randomization results in near-perfect balance within each region. Each subgroup has its own mini-randomization, which controls covariate imbalance by design.

We now simulate a scenario where both the treatment and the covariate west influence the outcome. Weâ€™ll compare block randomization to complete randomization across a range of sample sizes and track how much power we gain.

get_data <- function(
  N, 
  block = FALSE,
  effect_west = 2,
  effect_treatment = 1.5
) {
  # e.g. 80% of the people come from the west (1)
  west <- rbinom(N, 1, 0.8)
  # either block or complete randomization
  treatment <- if (block) block_ra(west) else complete_ra(N)
  # assign treatment and covariate effects
  outcome   <- effect_treatment * treatment + effect_west * west + rnorm(N)
  # return data
  data.frame(west, treatment, outcome)
}

run_test <- function(df) {
  # get power
  fit <- lm(outcome ~ treatment, data = df)
  pval <- summary(fit)$coefficients["treatment", "Pr(>|t|)"]
  pval < 0.05
}

run_sim <- function(N, block) {
  df <- get_data(N, block = block)
  run_test(df)
}

# Function to get estimated power + binomial CI
get_power_ci <- function(
  N, 
  block = FALSE,
  replications = 1000 
) {
  significant_results <- replicate(replications, run_sim(N, block))
  x <- sum(significant_results)
  pt <- prop.test(x, replications)             # approximate 95% CI
  tibble(
    N        = N,
    design   = if (block) "Block" else "Complete",
    power    = x / replications,
    lower    = pt$conf.int[1],
    upper    = pt$conf.int[2]
  )
}

# Run over N = 20 to 50
results <- map_dfr(seq(20,50,by=2), ~
  bind_rows(
    get_power_ci(.x, block = FALSE, replications = 1000),
    get_power_ci(.x, block =  TRUE, replications = 1000)
  )
)

This shows that block randomization can achieve desired power levels with fewer unitsâ€”a real advantage when resources are limited or units are hard to recruit.

This can even be further increased if we would have controlled for the variable west in the linear regression we used here to test whether the effect is significant or not.

Factorial Designs

A factorial design is an experimental setup in which two or more factors are varied simultaneously, allowing researchers to study not only the individual effects of each factor (main effects), but also their interactions. The most basic version is a 2Ã—2 factorial design, where each of two factors has two levels. This results in four unique combinations (2 Ã— 2 = 4).

Factorial designs are especially useful when:

  • You want to test multiple interventions at once,

  • You are interested in interaction effects (e.g., whether A is only effective when B is also present),

  • You want to maximize efficiency by getting multiple tests out of a single experiment.

Suppose youâ€™re testing how political messages influence public opinion. You vary on the one hand the political orientation of the message: left-leaning vs. right-leaning, and the strength of the message: soft vs. strong rhetoric:

df <- tibble(
    msg_strength = complete_ra(100, 40, conditions = c("weak", "strong") ), # 60:40
    msg_orientation = block_ra(msg_strength, conditions = c("left", "right"))  # 50:50
  )
with(df, table(msg_strength, msg_orientation))
            msg_orientation
msg_strength left right
      weak     30    30
      strong   20    20
Full vs. Fractional Factorial Designs

  • A full factorial design includes all possible combinations of the factors.

  • A (fractional) factorial design includes only a subset of combinations, often used when the total number of combinations is too large to be practical (e.g., in conjoint experiments or vignette studies).

Cluster Randomization

In many applied contexts, randomization must occur at the group or â€œclusterâ€ levelâ€”for example, assigning entire schools, villages, or social networks to a treatment condition rather than individual units. This often isnâ€™t the ideal from a statistical perspective. Ideally, we would randomize individuals, because this generally yields the highest statistical power. But in practice, thatâ€™s often not possible.

Letâ€™s consider education as an example. Suppose we want to measure learning outcomes in elementary school. While the outcomeâ€”test performanceâ€”is measured at the individual level (the student), the treatment might inherently apply to the entire group:

  • If a class gets an additional lesson per week, it affect the entire class.

  • If you give a teacher training, it spills over into every class the teacher teaches.

  • If an educational policy is implemented at the school or district level, it applies to all students within those units.

In such cases, individual-level randomization would not be feasible or meaningful. Instead, we randomize entire clustersâ€”like classes, schools, or even regions.

To understand how different randomization strategies affect assignmentâ€”and ultimately powerâ€”letâ€™s simulate a school system. We assume:

  • 4 schools
  • Each school has 4 classes
  • Each class has 4 students

Two Ways to Account for Clustering

  1. Aggregating to the Cluster Level

Instead of treating each student as a separate unit, we summarize outcomes at the school level:

t.test(outcome ~ treatment, 
       data = df_all |> 
         filter(design == "Cluster (School)") |> 
         group_by(school, treatment)  |>           # while it is useless to group by "treatment", it keeps the variable
         summarize(outcome = mean(outcome), .groups = "drop")
        )

Spillover Designs

While we talked about clustering and how it is sometimes not avoidable, sometimes it is and we want to do it anyways! This is the case when we assume sp called spillover effects. Spillovers (also called interference or externalities) occur when the treatment assigned to one unit influences the outcomes of other units. This violates a common assumption in experimental design called the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA), which states that a unitâ€™s potential outcomes depend only on its own treatment statusâ€”not on the treatment status of others.

Spillovers can arise in many contexts:

  • Education: A student receiving extra tutoring may affect classmates through peer learning.
  • Public health: Vaccinating some individuals may reduce transmission risk for unvaccinated individuals.
  • Politics: Exposing one person to a political campaign can influence friends or family they talk to.

But theyâ€™re not always bad! Sometimes estimating spillovers is the goal, and designs can be tailored to identify both direct and indirect effects.

If you suspect spillovers might exist, donâ€™t ignore themâ€”design around them with a two-stage randomization (or double randomization)**

Randomly assign:

  • Some clusters to complete treatment,
  • Some clusters to complete control,
  • And some clusters to mixed control and treatment.

This allows you to estimate:

  • Direct effects (treated vs untreated within treated clusters),
  • Spillover effects (untreated in treated clusters vs untreated in control clusters),
  • Total effects (treated in treated clusters vs untreated in control clusters).

Ethics of Randomization

Randomization is a foundational tool for identifying causal effects in experimental research. From a design perspective, it helps ensure that treatment assignment is not biased by unobserved factors. However, randomization also raises important ethical questions, particularly when it involves real-world interventions that affect peopleâ€™s lives.

When Is Randomization Ethical?

In the long term, randomization can be an ethical way to allocate limited resources. By rigorously testing which programs or policies are most effective, researchers help ensure that future investments deliver maximum benefit. Randomization also avoids allocation based on arbitrary or potentially biased criteria.

In the short term, randomization may be seen as a fair method when demand for a program exceeds supply. Instead of selecting beneficiaries based on subjective judgments, random assignment can distribute access in a transparent and impartial way.

When Is Randomization Problematic?

However, randomization is not always ethically neutral. Consider a program that provides loans. If we randomly assign loans to both low-income and high-income individuals, we may end up diverting resources away from those who need them most. If the treatment is already believed to be beneficial, denying it to vulnerable populations for the sake of experimentation can be ethically troubling.

In such cases, researchers must weigh the social value of the experiment against the potential harm. Where possible, designs should be structured to ultimately benefit all participants, including those initially assigned to control.

ðŸ§© Conclusion

Randomization is not just a technical procedureâ€”itâ€™s the backbone of credible causal inference. In this chapter, we explored a variety of randomization techniques, from simple and complete randomization to more advanced methods like block and cluster randomization, factorial designs, and waitlist approaches. Each method has its trade-offs, and understanding when and how to use them is crucial for designing effective experiments.

We saw that simple randomization, while easy to implement, can lead to imbalanceâ€”especially in smaller samplesâ€”reducing power. Block randomization improves balance on known covariates and can significantly increase statistical power without increasing sample size. Cluster randomization, though sometimes necessary, comes with a cost to power due to reduced unit independence. Factorial designs allow us to test multiple treatments efficiently, while spillover-aware designs help us understand broader causal dynamics in interconnected settings.

Importantly, we also discussed the ethical dimensions of random assignment. While randomization is often the fairest way to allocate scarce resources, it must be used responsibly and transparently.

Ultimately, good experimental design balances rigor, feasibility, and ethics. As researchers, our goal is not only to identify what works, but to do so in a way that is scientifically valid, practically useful, and ethically sound.

**Block randomization** (also known as **stratified randomization**) is a powerful method to ensure balance across treatment and control groups along key covariatesâ€”such as gender, age, or region. It is particularly helpful in small to moderate sample sizes, where **imbalance due to chance** can significantly reduce statistical power.

Imagine you want to test a treatment in Germany, where 80% of people live in the west and only 20% in the east. Suppose regional background influences the outcome you're measuring (e.g., political preferences or economic outlook). If you assign treatment using **complete randomization**, the eastern subgroupâ€”being smallâ€”could easily end up unevenly distributed between treatment and control. This would introduce **unnecessary variance** into your estimates, making it harder to detect a real effect.

With **block randomization**, you can avoid this issue by dividing units into blocks (e.g., by region) and **randomizing within each block**. This preserves random assignment while ensuring group balance on important covariatesâ€”thus improving precision and power.

Letâ€™s see this in action:

```{r}
df <- tibble(
    west       = rbinom(100, 1, 0.8),
    assignment = block_ra(west)
  )

with(df, table(assignment, west))
```

As you can see, block randomization results in near-perfect balance within each region. Each subgroup has its own mini-randomization, which controls covariate imbalance by design.

We now simulate a scenario where both the treatment and the covariate `west` influence the outcome. Weâ€™ll compare **block randomization** to **complete randomization** across a range of sample sizes and track how much power we gain.

```{r}
get_data <- function(
  N, 
  block = FALSE,
  effect_west = 2,
  effect_treatment = 1.5
) {
  # e.g. 80% of the people come from the west (1)
  west <- rbinom(N, 1, 0.8)
  # either block or complete randomization
  treatment <- if (block) block_ra(west) else complete_ra(N)
  # assign treatment and covariate effects
  outcome   <- effect_treatment * treatment + effect_west * west + rnorm(N)
  # return data
  data.frame(west, treatment, outcome)
}

run_test <- function(df) {
  # get power
  fit <- lm(outcome ~ treatment, data = df)
  pval <- summary(fit)$coefficients["treatment", "Pr(>|t|)"]
  pval < 0.05
}

run_sim <- function(N, block) {
  df <- get_data(N, block = block)
  run_test(df)
}

# Function to get estimated power + binomial CI
get_power_ci <- function(
  N, 
  block = FALSE,
  replications = 1000 
) {
  significant_results <- replicate(replications, run_sim(N, block))
  x <- sum(significant_results)
  pt <- prop.test(x, replications)             # approximate 95% CI
  tibble(
    N        = N,
    design   = if (block) "Block" else "Complete",
    power    = x / replications,
    lower    = pt$conf.int[1],
    upper    = pt$conf.int[2]
  )
}

# Run over N = 20 to 50
results <- map_dfr(seq(20,50,by=2), ~
  bind_rows(
    get_power_ci(.x, block = FALSE, replications = 1000),
    get_power_ci(.x, block =  TRUE, replications = 1000)
  )
)
```

Now letâ€™s plot estimated power across different sample sizes for both designs, including confidence intervals:

This simulation demonstrates how **block randomization consistently outperforms complete randomization**, especially when statistical power hovers around the 60â€“90% rangeâ€”precisely where most experiments aim to land.

-   Complete randomization: `r results |> filter(design == "Complete", power >= 0.8) |> pull(N) |> min()`

This shows that block randomization can achieve desired power levels **with fewer units**â€”a real advantage when resources are limited or units are hard to recruit.

This can even be further increased if we would have controlled for the variable `west` in the linear regression we used here to test whether the effect is significant or not.

## Factorial Designs

A **factorial design** is an experimental setup in which **two or more factors are varied simultaneously**, allowing researchers to study not only the **individual effects** of each factor (main effects), but also their **interactions**. The most basic version is a **2Ã—2 factorial design**, where each of two factors has two levels. This results in **four unique combinations** (2 Ã— 2 = 4).

Factorial designs are especially useful when:

-   You want to test multiple interventions at once,

-   You are interested in **interaction effects** (e.g., whether A is only effective when B is also present),

-   You want to maximize efficiency by getting multiple tests out of a single experiment.

Suppose you're testing how political messages influence public opinion. You vary on the one hand the political orientation of the message: *left-leaning* vs. *right-leaning*, and the strength of the message: *soft* vs. *strong* rhetoric:

```{r}
df <- tibble(
    msg_strength = complete_ra(100, 40, conditions = c("weak", "strong") ), # 60:40
    msg_orientation = block_ra(msg_strength, conditions = c("left", "right"))  # 50:50
  )
with(df, table(msg_strength, msg_orientation))
```

::: callout-note
### Full vs. Fractional Factorial Designs

-   A **full factorial design** includes **all possible combinations** of the factors.

-   A (**fractional**) **factorial design** includes **only a subset** of combinations, often used when the total number of combinations is too large to be practical (e.g., in conjoint experiments or vignette studies).
:::

## Cluster Randomization

In many applied contexts, **randomization must occur at the group or â€œclusterâ€ level**â€”for example, assigning entire schools, villages, or social networks to a treatment condition rather than individual units. This often isnâ€™t the ideal from a statistical perspective. Ideally, we would randomize **individuals**, because this generally yields the **highest statistical power**. But in practice, thatâ€™s often not possible.

Letâ€™s consider education as an example. Suppose we want to measure **learning outcomes** in elementary school. While the outcomeâ€”test performanceâ€”is measured at the individual level (the student), the **treatment** might inherently apply to the entire group:

-   If a class gets an additional lesson per week, it affect the **entire class**.

-   If you give a teacher training, it spills over into **every class** the teacher teaches.

-   If an educational policy is implemented at the **school** or **district** level, it applies to all students within those units.

In such cases, individual-level randomization would not be feasible or meaningful. Instead, we randomize entire **clusters**â€”like classes, schools, or even regions.

To understand how different randomization strategies affect assignmentâ€”and ultimately powerâ€”letâ€™s simulate a school system. We assume:

-   4 schools
-   Each school has 4 classes
-   Each class has 4 students


Letâ€™s assume that:

We now apply several different randomization designs:

Although clustering may be necessary, it comes at a **cost to statistical power**. Why? Because observations within clusters are **not independent**. If a teacher affects all their students, then those studentsâ€™ outcomes are correlatedâ€”not independent replicates.

Letâ€™s see the consequences of this. Suppose we analyze the "Cluster (School)" design with a standard t-test:

```{r}
t.test(outcome ~ treatment, data = df_all |> filter(design == "Cluster (School)"))
```

We get a highly significant result ($p < 0.001$)â€”but this is misleading. The test treats all 64 students as independent, which violates assumptions.

### Two Ways to Account for Clustering

1.  **Aggregating to the Cluster Level**

Instead of treating each student as a separate unit, we summarize outcomes at the school level:

```{r}
t.test(outcome ~ treatment, 
       data = df_all |> 
         filter(design == "Cluster (School)") |> 
         group_by(school, treatment)  |>           # while it is useless to group by "treatment", it keeps the variable
         summarize(outcome = mean(outcome), .groups = "drop")
        )
```

Now, the test is no longer significant (p = 0.151). We reduced pseudo-replication by correctly using the cluster as the unit of analysis. But we also lost power because we only have 4 clusters (schools).

2.  **Using Hierarchical Models (e.g., Mixed Effects)**

An alternative is to use a random effects or multilevel model that accounts for the hierarchical structure:

```{r}
library(lme4)

fit <- lmer(outcome ~ treatment + (1 | school), data = df_all |> filter(design == "Cluster (School)"))
summary(fit)
```

This approach retains individual-level data, while correctly modeling the correlation within clusters.

Another problem is that if the clusters differ **systematically** in ways that affect the outcomeâ€”say, wealthier schools tend to be assigned more likely to the treatmentâ€”we risk **bias** or inflated variance unless properly controlled for. If you look at @fig-random-designs the cluster assignment for schools would not allow us to differentiate between treatment effect and effects of school 1 & 3.

### Spillover Designs

While we talked about clustering and how it is sometimes not avoidable, sometimes it is and we want to do it anyways! This is the case when we assume sp called **spillover effects**. **Spillovers** (also called **interference** or **externalities**) occur when the treatment assigned to one unit influences the **outcomes of other units**. This violates a common assumption in experimental design called the **Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (SUTVA)**, which states that a unitâ€™s potential outcomes depend only on its own treatment statusâ€”not on the treatment status of others.

Spillovers can arise in many contexts:

-   **Education**: A student receiving extra tutoring may affect classmates through peer learning.
-   **Public health**: Vaccinating some individuals may reduce transmission risk for unvaccinated individuals.
-   **Politics**: Exposing one person to a political campaign can influence friends or family they talk to.

But theyâ€™re not always bad! Sometimes **estimating spillovers is the goal**, and designs can be tailored to **identify both direct and indirect effects**.

If you suspect spillovers might exist, donâ€™t ignore themâ€”design around them with a two-stage randomization (or double randomization)\*\*

Randomly assign:

-   Some clusters to complete treatment,
-   Some clusters to complete control,
-   And some clusters to mixed control and treatment.

This allows you to estimate:

-   **Direct effects** (treated vs untreated within treated clusters),
-   **Spillover effects** (untreated in treated clusters vs untreated in control clusters),
-   **Total effects** (treated in treated clusters vs untreated in control clusters).

# Ethics of Randomization

**Randomization** is a foundational tool for identifying causal effects in experimental research. From a design perspective, it helps ensure that treatment assignment is not biased by unobserved factors. However, **randomization also raises important ethical questions**, particularly when it involves real-world interventions that affect people's lives.

### When Is Randomization Ethical?

In the **long term**, randomization can be an ethical way to allocate limited resources. By rigorously testing which programs or policies are most effective, researchers help ensure that future investments deliver maximum benefit. Randomization also avoids allocation based on arbitrary or potentially biased criteria.

In the **short term**, randomization may be seen as a fair method when demand for a program exceeds supply. Instead of selecting beneficiaries based on subjective judgments, random assignment can distribute access in a transparent and impartial way.

### When Is Randomization Problematic?

However, randomization is not always ethically neutral. Consider a program that provides loans. If we randomly assign loans to both low-income and high-income individuals, we may end up **diverting resources away from those who need them most**. If the treatment is already believed to be beneficial, denying it to vulnerable populations for the sake of experimentation can be ethically troubling.

In such cases, **researchers must weigh the social value of the experiment against the potential harm**. Where possible, designs should be structured to **ultimately benefit all participants**, including those initially assigned to control.

# ðŸ§© Conclusion

Randomization is not just a technical procedureâ€”itâ€™s the backbone of credible causal inference. In this chapter, we explored a variety of randomization techniques, from simple and complete randomization to more advanced methods like block and cluster randomization, factorial designs, and waitlist approaches. Each method has its trade-offs, and understanding when and how to use them is crucial for designing effective experiments.

We saw that **simple randomization**, while easy to implement, can lead to imbalanceâ€”especially in smaller samplesâ€”reducing power. **Block randomization** improves balance on known covariates and can significantly increase statistical power without increasing sample size. **Cluster randomization**, though sometimes necessary, comes with a cost to power due to reduced unit independence. **Factorial designs** allow us to test multiple treatments efficiently, while **spillover-aware designs** help us understand broader causal dynamics in interconnected settings.

Importantly, we also discussed the **ethical dimensions** of random assignment. While randomization is often the fairest way to allocate scarce resources, it must be used responsibly and transparently.

Ultimately, good experimental design balances **rigor, feasibility, and ethics**. As researchers, our goal is not only to identify what works, but to do so in a way that is scientifically valid, practically useful, and ethically sound.
